How much of the work space can be claimed

Simplified option

To simplify the claim for your work space and office supply expenses, you can claim a flat rate of $x on your 2020 tax return.

To determine the amount of the home expenses you can claim for your work space, the expenses should be separated between the employment use and the non-employment use of the home. To calculate the reasonable percentage, you need to consider the size and the use of the room.

Size of the home and work space

Size of home

All finished areas within the home count towards the size of the home. This includes:

  • hallways
  • bathrooms
  • kitchen

You can measure in square metres or square feet.

Size of work space

The reasonable portion of the work space expenses that you can claim is based on the size of the room where your work space is located.

You can measure in square metres or square feet.

Example: Working from the dining room table

Due to COVID-19, Sam has been working from home using her dining room table. The dining room represents 12% of the total square footage of her house and is used for work for 40 hours out of a total 168 hours in the week.

Since the room is not used only for work, Sam will need to calculate the employment use of that work space.

  •    12% (size of work space compared to the entire finished area of the home)
  • times 23.8% (40 hours worked per week divided by 168 hours in a week)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals     2.8% (percentage of home that is used as a work space)

If she paid $1,200 for electricity, heat, and water for the period she worked at home because of COVID-19, the employment use portion is $1,200 x 2.8% = $33.6

If you have an open concept design, you would use a reasonable percentage of the open space to claim as your work space. You cannot use the entire floor space to represent your work space.

Determine the reasonable percentage of your home that is used as a work space

To determine the percentage of your home that is used as a work space is, use this calculation:

Size of work space divided by Size of home times 100 equals % of work space in the home

For example:

  •    40 m2 (size of work space)
  • divided by 400 m2 (size of home)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals    10% (percentage of home that is used as a work space)

The calculator will perform this calculation for you.

Types of work spaces

There are two types of work spaces:

  • Common (shared) area
  • Designated room

Common (shared) area: Option 1 of 2

A common area is a space that has other purposes besides your work (for example, working at a kitchen table or using the family computer room). This space can only be claimed for the hours it is used for employment purposes.

Designated room: Option 2 of 2

A designated room is a space designated solely for your employment duties (for example, a home office).

Hours per week you can claim

The hours you can claim are based on whether the space is a common (shared) area or exclusively dedicated as a work space.

Common (shared) area: Option 1 of 2

This space can only be claimed for the hours it is used for employment purposes. For example, if you work 40 hours a week at the kitchen table:

  •    40 hours (hours worked)
  • divided by 168 hours (total hours in a week)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals 23.8% (percentage of time you can claim for the work space)

Designated room: Option 2 of 2

You can claim the full 24 hours per day, 7 days per week, for the use of that room when calculating the work space. For example, if you work 40 hours a week in your dedicated room:

  •    40 hours (hours worked)
  • equals 100% (percentage of time you can claim for the work space)

The calculator will perform this calculation for you.

Number of work spaces and workers in the home

If only one person has a work space

If only one person in the home uses a work space, that person will claim the expenses.

If multiple people are working in the same home but in different work spaces

If multiple people are working from your home, each in their own work space, they each calculate and claim the expenses separately.

If multiple people are sharing the same work space

If multiple people are sharing the same work space in your home, there are two options:

Sharing the claim: Option 1 of 2

The amount of the claim can be divided up and shared between the workers, but the individual claims cannot exceed the total claim amount.

One person claims: Option 1 of 2

One person claims the full amount.

Examples

Working in a designated room

Chris lives in a 7 room house and has a designated area in the home where Chris only performs employment duties.

  •  20 m2 (area of Chris' work space in his home)
  • divided by 200 m2 (total finished area)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals 10% (percentage of home Chris uses for work space)

If Chris was sharing this work space with someone (Danielle) who was also using the room as a dedicated work space, this gives them two options:

  • One person claims
  • Both claim

One person claims: Option 1 of 2

Chris or Danielle could claim 10% of the work-space-in-the-home expenses such as water and electricity.

Both claim: Option 2 of 2

Chris and Danielle could also share the claim but would only be able to claim a total of 10% of the expenses between the two of them.

Working in a common (shared) area

Sam and Terry rent an 8 room house. Due to COVID-19, they have been working from home using their dining room table.

  •  10 m2 (area used for work space in the home)
  • divided by 120 m2 (total finished area)
  • times 100 (converting into a percentage)
  • equals 12% (percentage of home you use for work space)

If 40 hours a week are spent working at the dining table:

  • 12% (percentage of home used for work space)
  • times 23.8% (40 hours worked divided by 168 total hours in a week)
  • equals 2.8%

This gives them two options:

  • One person claims
  • Both claim

One person claims: Option 1 of 2

Sam or Terry could claim 2.8% of the work-space-in-the-home expenses such as water and electricity.

Both claim: Option 2 of 2

Sam and Terry could also share the claim but would only be able to claim a total of 2.8% of the expenses between the two of them.
