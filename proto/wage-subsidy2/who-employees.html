Which of your employees qualify

When applying for CEWS, you will need to know which of your employees can be included in your calculation. You will also need to know how much their pay (eligible remuneration) was.

Who are eligible employees

An eligible employee is a person who was employed by you (the eligible employer) in Canada during the claim period.

For claim periods 1 to 4, you can’t include employees who had 14 or more consecutive unpaid days in the period. However, for periods 5 and later, you no longer have to exclude these employees from your calculation.

Employee eligibility depends on the person being employed in Canada, not living in Canada

Retroactively hiring and paying employees

Employees you laid off can become eligible retroactively, as long as you rehire and pay them so they meet the eligibility criteria for the claim period. You must rehire and pay these employees before you include them in your subsidy claim.

Eligible employees and the Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB)

People you rehired may have received, or continue to receive, the Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB). Depending on the specific situation, these employees may be required to repay some or all of the CERB they received.

CERB recipients who already know they will need to repay the CERB can find out how to return or repay the benefit.

Non-arm’s-length and arm’s-length eligible employees

You will be asked whether an employee is arm’s length or not when you calculate your subsidy amount, because the CEWS is calculated differently for eligible non-arm’s-length and arm’s-length employees.

In a small business, a non-arm’s-length employee would most commonly be:

  • a business owner
  • a member of an owner’s immediate family (for example, a spouse, parent, child, sibling, grandparent, or grandchild)

It’s possible (but less common) for a non-arm’s-length employee relationship to exist between unrelated people, depending on the circumstances.

For most small businesses, workers are usually arm’s-length employees unless they are an owner or an owner’s family member.

If you’re not sure about whether an employee is considered arm’s length or non-arm’s length, or have a business involving more complex control (like a corporation, trust, or partnership), you can read more about specific relationships that are considered non-arm’s length.

Active employees and employees on leave with pay

Whether an eligible employee is active or on leave with pay is determined one week at a time in the subsidy calculator.

Active employees
Are employees who worked for any part of that week during the claim period.
Employees on leave with pay
Are those who received full or partial pay but did not work at all during the week. (In some government documents this has also been referred to as “furloughed” employees.)

For claim periods 5 and later, employees who were on leave with pay for a full week or more are treated differently than actively working employees when calculating your subsidy amount. The amount for employees in either situation from week to week is determined automatically when you use the online calculator or spreadsheet.

Or, you can read the in-depth information about how the wage subsidy is calculated for employees on leave with pay.

For all claim periods, you can claim a 100% refund of the employer-paid part of contributions made on behalf of any eligible employees who are on leave with pay for any full week within the claim period.

The employer-paid contributions that can be refunded for employees on leave with pay for the week include:

  • Employment Insurance (EI)
  • the Quebec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP)
  • the Canada Pension Plan (CPP)
  • the Quebec Pension Plan (QPP)

Eligible employees participating in a Work-Sharing benefit program

If you are participating in Employment and Social Development Canada's Work-Sharing benefit program, you will need to know the amount of EI benefits received by your eligible employees through the program during the claim period.

If you don’t have the exact amount of work-sharing benefits received by your employees, we will accept a reasonable estimate.

Determining employee pay (eligible remuneration)

Eligible remuneration is normally the type of employee pay you would make payroll deductions on.

Eligible revenue includes amounts such as:

  • salary
  • wages
  • certain taxable benefits
  • fees and commissions

Eligible revenue does not include amounts such as:

  • severance pay
  • stock option benefits
  • dividends
  • tips customers gave directly to the employee, or
  • the personal use of a corporate vehicle
Read more about specific items included in eligible remuneration

Claim period pay per week

For all eligible employees, you will need to know how much eligible remuneration you paid them for each week in the claim period.

If your pay schedule doesn’t align with the wage subsidy claim period dates, you will have to manually calculate how much you paid the employee for each week. It doesn’t matter whether the employee receives their paycheque at the end of the week, at the end of the month, or another time, as long as you use the actual amount they were paid that relates to that week. You can’t use an average of daily wages.

If you pay employees weekly or bi-weekly, you can use the calculator spreadsheet to enter your employees’ pay.

Pre-crisis pay (baseline remuneration)

Pre-crisis pay (baseline remuneration) is an average amount of eligible remuneration the employee received per week during a specific period before March 15.

The pre-crisis pay periods you may use when calculating an employee’s pre-crisis pay change depending on the claim period. To get the most potential benefit, choose the pre-crisis pay period that has the highest weekly average for that employee.

[table caption goes here]
Claim period Pre-crisis pay period options
Periods 1 to 3
  • January 1 to March 15, 2020 (75 days)
  • March 1 to May 31, 2019 (92 days)
Period 4
  • 1 to March 15, 2020 (75 days)
  • March 1 to May 31, 2019 (92 days
  • March 1, 2019, to June 30, 2019 (122 days)
Periods 5 and later
  • January 1 to March 15, 2020 (75 days)
  • July 1, 2019, to December 31, 2019 (184 days)

These choices (other than January 1 to March 15, 2020) were not available when the CEWS was first introduced, but are retroactive to claim period 1. If you want to, you can change a previous claim you submitted. New

For claim periods 1 to 6 you need a pre-crisis pay amount for all employees you’re including in your claims.

For claim periods 7 and later, you only need a pre-crisis pay amount for any employees who were either:

  • on leave with pay for a week in the claim period
  • non-arm’s length employees

Calculate the pre-crisis pay amount

  1. Total all the remuneration paid to the employee during the chosen pre-crisis pay period
  2. Divide that total pay by the number of days in the chosen pre-crisis pay period, minus any period of 7 or more consecutive days the employee was not paid for
  3. Multiply that result by 7 to get the weekly average
Example of choosing an employee’s pre-crisis pay amount

ADC Ltd. is preparing to claim the subsidy for period 5. Their employee Issa was paid $8,000 from January 1 to March 15, 2020, and $21,000 from July 1, 2019, to December 21, 2019. Issa was off work without pay from December 2, 2019, to January 15, 2020 (inclusive).

Calculating Issa’s pre-crisis pay for January 1 to March 15, 2020 (pre-crisis pay period option 1)

  • Total pay for this period: $8000
  • Days in claim period (75) minus consecutive days in this period without pay (15) = 60
  • Weekly average using pre-crisis pay period option 1: ($8000/60)*7=$933.33

Calculating Issa’s pre-crisis pay for July 1 to December 21, 2019 (pre-crisis pay period option 2)

  • Total pay for this period = $21,000
  • Days in claim period (184) minus consecutive days without pay (30) = 154
  • Weekly average using pre-crisis pay period option 2: ($21,000/154)*7 = $954.55

ADC Ltd. should use pre-crisis pay period of July 1, 2019, to December 31, 2019 (option 2) for Issa because it results in the higher weekly average.
