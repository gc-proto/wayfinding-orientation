Tourism and Hospitality Recovery Program (THRP)

As a business, charity, or non-profit in Canada who has been affected by the COVID-19 pandemic, you may be eligible for a wage subsidy, a rent subsidy, or both through the THRP.

You may qualify if you are either:

  • part of the tourism, hospitality, arts, entertainment, or recreation sectors, or
  • affected by a qualifying public health restriction

Who can apply for the THRP

There are two ways eligible organizations can qualify for the THRP:

Tourism, hospitality, arts, entertainment, and recreation sectors: Option 1 of 2

The first way to qualify for the THRP is by meeting the following three conditions:

  • More than 50% of your total eligible revenue for the prior reference periods for claim periods 1 to 13 (but excluding one of either claim period 10 or 11) comes from one or more of the tourism, hospitality, arts, entertainment, or recreation activities this program supports
  • You have a 12-month average revenue drop from March 2020 to February 2021 of at least 40%
  • You have a claim period revenue drop of at least 40%

Find out if your activities are considered part of tourism, hospitality, arts, entertainment, or recreation

Organizations subject to a qualifying public health restriction or qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction: Option 2 of 2

The second way to qualify for the THRP was introduced as the Local Lockdown Program. It is available to eligible organizations, regardless of sector.

The conditions to qualify depend on which claim period you are applying for.

For claim periods 24 and 25 (December 19, 2021, to February 12, 2022), you must meet the following two conditions:

  • You were subject to either a qualifying public health restriction or a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction
  • You have a claim period revenue drop of at least 25%

For claim periods 22, 23 and 26 to 28, you must meet the following two conditions:

  • You were subject to a qualifying public health restriction
  • You have a claim period revenue drop of at least 40%

Support in the event of public health restrictions

If you are not a qualifying tourism or hospitality entity, your business, charity or non-profit may be eligible for a subsidy under the Tourism and Hospitality Recovery Program (THRP) if you are subject to a:

  • qualifying public health restriction, or
  • qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction

To qualify for the THRP through this option, you must meet all of the following:

For claim periods 22, 23 and 26 to 28:

  1. The conditions that relate to a public health restriction
  2. Two additional conditions that relate to a qualifying public health restriction
  3. A minimum 40% revenue drop for the claim period you are applying for

For claim periods 24 and 25 only:

  • The conditions that relate respectively to a public health restriction or to a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction
  • Two additional conditions that relate respectively to a qualifying public health restriction or a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction
  • A minimum 25% revenue drop for the claim period you are applying for

Public health restriction and partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction

Both a public health restriction and a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction are orders that meet certain conditions.

Many of these conditions are the same for both types of health restriction. They both must:

  • be based on an order or decision issued by a federal, provincial, or municipal government, or a local health authority in response to the COVID-19 pandemic
  • be limited in scope based on one or more factors such as:
    • geographical boundaries
    • type of business or other activity
    • risks associated with a particular location
  • result in sanctions or be an offence if you do not comply

In addition, there are different conditions that need to be met for each type of health restriction:

Examples

How the 7 day criteria works for a public health restriction and a qualifying public health restriction

A public health restriction requires that, as a result of the order, some or all of your (or your non-arm’s length tenant’s) regular business activities at a qualifying property have stopped for at least:

  • 7 days in a row, but
  • the days can span across 2 different claim periods.

In addition, Condition 1 of a qualifying public health restriction requires that you have at least one qualifying property that is subject to a public health restriction for at least:

  • 7 days in the same claim period, but
  • the days do not need to be in a row.

This means that to meet this criterion in a claim period:

  1. you had at least one qualifying property that was subject to a public health restriction during the claim period, and
  2. some or all of the regular business activities at or in connection with the qualifying property had stopped for at least 7 days in the claim period.

Example – Condition 1 of a qualifying public health restriction

Kim’s tailor shop owns 4 qualifying properties.Kim operates a hairdressing business and has 4 different locations, with each location being a qualifying property.

On two recent occasions, an order required all of the hairdressing activities at two of Kim’s locations to stop. Since hairdressing activities account for all of the activities at the first affected location, Kim chose to close that location. However, Kim kept the second affected location open for sales of haircare products, since she traditionally earned some revenue from those sales.

How the 7-day criterion works

Step 1: Determine if the two locations meet the 7-day criterion for the purpose of a public health restriction

In both instances, each affected location met all the other conditions required to be subject to a public health restriction.

Dates the hairdressing activities were required to stop:

  • October 18 to October 27, 2021, inclusive (10 days in a row which span claim periods 21 and 22), and
  • November 18 to November 24, 2021, inclusive (7 days in a row which span claim periods 22 and 23)

This means that, on two occasions, some or all of the activities at each of these two locations were stopped for 7 days in a row or more.

As a result, each of the two qualifying properties meet the condition that some or all activities at (or in connection with) the qualifying property were stopped for at least 7 days in a row for the purpose of a public health restriction.

Step 2: Determine if the two locations meet the 7-day criterion for the purpose of a qualifying public health restriction

Two of Kim’s locations had to stop all of their activities (close) twice recently due to public health restrictions.

Dates they were closed:

  1. October 18 to October 27, 2021, inclusive (10 days in a row), and
  2. November 18 to November 24, 2021, inclusive (7 days in a row)

Claim period 22:

  • started on October 24, 2021, and
  • ended on November 20, 2021

Days the hairdressing activities were required to stop in claim period 22:

  • 4 days at the beginning of period 22 (October 24 to 27, inclusive), and
  • 3 days at the end of period 22 (November 18 to 20, inclusive)

This means that some or all of the activities at each of these two locations were required to stop (as a result of an order or decision) for a total of 7 days during claim period 22.

Conclusion:

Kim’s hairdressing business meets Condition 1 of a qualifying public health restriction in claim period 22 because:

  • one or more qualifying properties were subject to a public health restriction for at least 7 days in the claim period.

This means that Kim’s tailor shop in each of those locations were closed for a total of 7 days during claim period 22:

  • 4 days at the beginning of period 22 (October 24 to 27, inclusive), and
  • 3 days at the end of period 22 (November 18 to 20, inclusive)

Kim’s tailor shop business meets condition 1 of a qualifying public health restriction in claim period 22 because:

  • one or more public health restrictions experienced by two of the qualifying properties lasted for at least 7 days in a row, and
  • each were closed due to public health restrictions for at least 7 days in the claim period.
How the 7 day criteria works for a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction and a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction

A partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction requires that, as a result of an order or decision that meets certain conditions, some or all of your—or your non-arm’s length tenant’s—activities at (or in connection with) a qualifying property have been reduced by means of capacity or similar restriction by 50% or more for:

  • at least 7 days in a row, but
  • the days can span 2 different claim periods.

In addition, Condition 1 of a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction requires that you—or your non-arm’s length tenants—have at least one qualifying property that is subject to a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction for:

  • at least 7 days in the claim period, but
  • the days do not need to be in a row.

Example – Condition 1 of a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction

Jan operates a corporate training business and has 4 different locations where she exclusively offers in-person training events, with each location being a qualifying property.

On two recent occasions, an order required a 50% reduction in capacity for in-person training events at two of Jan’s locations.

How the 7-day criterion works

Step 1: Determine if the two locations meet the 7-day criterion for the purpose of a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction

In both instances, each affected location met all the other conditions required to be subject to a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction.

Dates the capacity was reduced at the two affected locations:

  • December 13 to December 22, 2021, inclusive (10 days in a row which span claim periods 23 and 24), and
  • January 12 to January 18, 2022, inclusive (7 days in a row which span claim periods 24 and 25)

This means that, on two occasions, the activities at each of these locations were affected by a 50% reduction in capacity for 7 days in a row or more.

As a result, each of the two qualifying properties meet the condition that some or all activities at (or in connection with) the qualifying property were reduced due to capacity reductions or similar restrictions by 50% or more for at least 7 days in a row for the purpose of a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction.

Step 2: Determine if the two locations meet the 7-day criterion for the purpose of a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction

Claim period 24:

  • started on December 19, 2021
  • ended on January 15, 2022

Days the capacity was reduced for in-person training events at the two affected locations in claim period 24:

  • 4 days at the beginning of period 24 (December 19 to 22, inclusive), and
  • 4 days at the end of period 24 (January 12 to 15, inclusive)

This means that the 50% reductions in capacity affected the in-person training events at each of these two locations for a total of 8 days each during claim period 24.

Conclusion:

Jan’s corporate training business meets Condition 1 of a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction in claim period 24 because:

  • one or more qualifying properties were subject to a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction for at least 7 days in the claim period.

How the 25% revenue "test" works if you have more than one qualifying property criterion works for a public health restriction and a qualifying public health restriction

A public health restriction requires that at least approximately 25% of your—or your non-arm’s length tenant’s—qualifying revenues earned in the prior reference period from (or in connection with) the qualifying property were derived from the activities that were required to stop as a result of an order or decision.

In addition, Condition 2 of a qualifying public health restriction requires that at least approximately 25% of your total eligible revenue (together with the eligible revenue of your non-arm’s length tenants) earned in the prior reference period came from the activities that were required to stop as a result of an order or decision. You must consider the eligible revenue in relation to the stopped activities against the eligible revenue from all your qualifying properties, even if some of your qualifying properties were subject to a public health restriction and others were not.

Example: Condition 2 of a qualifying public health restriction

Kim operates a hairdressing business and has 4 different locations, with each location being a qualifying property.

On two recent occasions, an order required all of the hairdressing activities at two of Kim’s locations to stop. Since hairdressing activities account for all of the activities at the first affected location, Kim chose to close that location. However, Kim kept the second affected location open for sales of haircare products, since she traditionally earned some revenue from those sales. In both instances, each affected location met all the conditions required to be subject to a public health restriction.

How the 25% revenue criterion works

Step 1: Determine if each of the two locations meet the 25% revenue criterion for the purpose of a public health restriction

For the purpose of a public health restriction, the 25% revenue criterion is determined on a qualifying property by qualifying property basis. This means that the activities that were required to stop have to account for at least 25% of the eligible revenues earned during the prior reference period from (or in connection with) the affected qualifying property.

For each affected location, the eligible revenue earned in the prior reference period for claim period 22 (November 2019) was $25,000.

For the first affected location, the activities that were required to stop accounted for 100% of the eligible revenues earned ($25,000/$25,000 = 100%) during the prior reference period.

For the second affected location, the activities that were required to stop accounted for $15,000, while the sale of hair care products accounted for the remaining $10,000. Accordingly, 60% of the eligible revenues earned ($15,000/$25,000 = 60%) during the prior reference period were from the activities that were required to stop.

In each case, the activities that were required to stop account for at least 25% of the eligible revenues earned during the prior reference period from (or in connection with) the affected qualifying property. As a result, each of the two qualifying properties meet the 25% revenue criterion for the purpose of a public health restriction.

Step 2: Determine if the entity meets the 25% revenue criterion for the purpose of a qualifying public health restriction

For the purpose of a qualifying public health restriction, the 25% revenue criterion is based on the entity’s overall eligible revenues. This means that the activities that were required to stop at the locations subject to a public health restriction account for at least approximately 25% of your total eligible revenue (together with the eligible revenue of your non-arm's length tenants) earned during the prior reference period.

As mentioned in Step 1, the eligible revenue earned in the prior reference period for claim period 22 (November 2019) was $25,000 for each affected location.

The eligible revenue earned in the prior reference period from Kim’s other two locations (i.e. her locations not subject to a public health restriction) was also $25,000 each. Accordingly, the total eligible revenue earned by Kim’s hairdressing business in the prior reference period for claim period 22 (November 2019) was $100,000.

For the first affected location (i.e. the location that Kim chose to close), the revenue from the activities that were required to stop accounted for 25% ($25,000/$100,000) of the total eligible revenue of Kim’s hairdressing business in the prior reference period for claim period 22.

For the second affected location (i.e. the location that Kim chose to keep open), the revenue from the activities that were required to stop accounted for 15% ($15,000/$100,000) of the total eligible revenue of the business in the prior reference period for claim period 22.

Together they account for 40% (25% + 15%) of the total eligible revenue of Kim’s hairdressing business earned during the prior reference period.

This means that at least approximately 25% of the total eligible revenue of the business in the prior reference period came from the activities that were required to stop.

Conclusion:

Kim’s hair salon business meets Condition 2 for a qualifying public health restriction in claim period 22 because:

  • the activities that were required to stop at the two qualifying properties that were subject to a public health restriction account for at least approximately 25% of its total eligible revenue earned by the business during the prior reference period.

At least one of your qualifying properties must be subject to a qualifying public health restriction.

If some of your qualifying properties were subject to a qualifying public health restriction and others are not, you must demonstrate that at least 25% of your total eligible revenue (together with the eligible revenue of your non-arm’s length tenant) during the prior reference period came from the qualifying properties that were closed or restricted.

Examples: Condition 2 of a qualifying public health restriction

Example 1: Kim’s tailor shop meets condition 2

Kim’s tailor shop business has 2 qualifying properties that were closed by public health restrictions that lasted for 7 days in a row in claim period 22. The other 2 qualifying properties remained open for business as usual.

When Kim looks at the total eligible revenue of the business, they determine that each of the 2 qualifying properties that were closed by public health restrictions accounted for 12.5% of the total eligible revenue for their business when compared to the prior reference period for claim period 22.

(12.5%) + (12.5%) = 25%

Kim’s business meets condition 2 for a qualifying public health restriction because their qualifying properties that were closed due to a public health restriction account for 25% of the total eligible revenue.

Example 2: Sanjay’s retail business does not meet condition 2

Sanjay’s retail business owns 7 qualifying properties.

Sanjay has 2 qualifying properties that were subject to a public health restriction and closed for 7 days in a row in claim period 22. Because of this, Sanjay’s retail business meets condition 1 for a qualifying public health restriction.

When Sanjay looks at their total eligible revenue, they determine that the locations that were closed by a public health restriction accounted for only 15% of the total eligible revenue for their business when compared to the prior reference period for claim period 22.

Sanjay’s retail business does not meet condition 2 for a qualifying public health restriction because the qualifying properties that were closed due to a public health restriction do not account for 25% of Sanjay’s total eligible revenue when compared to the prior reference period for claim period 22.

How the 50% revenue criterion works for a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction and a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction

A partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction requires that, as a result of a order or decision that meets certain conditions, some or all of your—or your non-arm’s length tenant’s—activities at (or in connection with) the qualifying property are reduced by means of capacity or similar restrictions by 50% or more, but there is no revenue criterion that must be met to be subject to a partial public health restriction.

However , Condition 2 of a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction requires that the activities that were required to be reduced due to capacity or similar restrictions at the qualifying properties that were subject to a partial (capacity-limited) public health restriction account for at least 50% of your total eligible revenue (together with the eligible revenue of your non-arm's length tenants) earned during the prior reference period.

This means you must consider the eligible revenue in relation to the reduced activities against the eligible revenue from all your qualifying properties, even if some of your qualifying properties were subject to a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction and others were not.

Example: Condition 2 of a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction

Jan operates an in-person corporate training business and has 4 different locations, with each location being a qualifying property.

On two recent occasions, an order required a 50% reduction in capacity at two of Jan’s locations. In both instances, each affected location met all the conditions required to be subject to a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction.

Jan’s remaining two locations were not subject to a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction and could operate at full capacity.

The eligible revenue earned in the prior reference period for claim period 24 (January 2020) from in-person training events at the two locations that were subject to the partial (capacity-limiting) public health restrictions was $100,000 ($50,000 per location) and the eligible revenue from the remaining two locations was $60,000 ($30,000 per location). Accordingly, the total eligible revenue earned by Jan’s in-person corporate training business in the prior reference period was $160,000.

For the purpose of a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction, the activities that were reduced at the locations subject to a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction must account for at least approximately 50% of your total eligible revenue (together with the eligible revenue of your non-arm's length tenants) earned during the prior reference period.

The activities that were reduced at each of the 2 qualifying properties subject to a partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction accounted for 31.25% ($50,000/$160,000) of the total eligible revenue of Jan’s corporate training business in the prior reference period for claim period 24. Together they account for 62.50% (31.25% + 31.25%) of the total eligible revenue of the business earned during the prior reference period.

Conclusion:

Jan’s corporate training business meets Condition 2 for a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction in claim period 24 because:

  • the activities that were reduced due to capacity or similar restrictions at the two locations that were subject to the partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction account for at least approximately 50% of its total eligible revenue earned during the prior reference period.

Subsidies you may qualify for under the THRP

Through the THRP, whether you are considered a qualifying tourism or hospitality entity or you were subject to a qualifying public health restriction or a qualifying partial (capacity-limiting) public health restriction, you may be able to get a subsidy to help cover wages (formerly the Canada Emergency Wage Subsidy) and rent (formerly the Canada Emergency Rent Subsidy).

You may also qualify for the Canada Recovery Hiring Program (CRHP).
